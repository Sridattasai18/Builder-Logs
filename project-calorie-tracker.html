If I want to build a calorie tracker for myself, what should I need to know?

AI

The Real Challenges

  • Data is the hard part, not the UI. You need a food database — 300,000+ foods. Build your own is nearly impossible. Use Open Food Facts (free), Nutritionix, or USDA FoodData Central.
  • Logging is friction-heavy. The #1 reason people quit calorie apps is the tedium. Consider: barcode scanning, meal templates, voice input, or AI photo recognition.
  • The math needs to be right. Calculate each user's daily calorie goal from age, weight, height, gender, and activity level — this is called TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure).
  • Very crowded market. MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, Lose It are free and huge. You need a clear reason someone picks yours.

What You'd Actually Need to Build

  • User profile (age, weight, height, goal)
  • Daily calorie target calculator
  • Food search connected to a real database
  • Meal logging (breakfast, lunch, dinner, snacks)
  • Daily progress view (calories in vs. target)
  • History & trends over time
💡 If it's just for you — totally worth building as a learning project. If you want others to use it — it needs real investment in data, UX, and a clear reason to exist.

AI

1. Best Open Source Model for Indian Food

No single model is great at Indian food out of the box — this is a real gap. Here's the ranking:

  • Llama 3 / 3.1 (Meta) — Best general knowledge. Decent on popular dishes (dal, biryani, dosa, paneer) but struggles with regional specifics like Chettinad or Konkani cooking.
  • Gemma 2 (Google) — Slightly better on Indian food due to Google's India-specific training data, but still not deep on regional variety.
  • Mistral — Good reasoning, average Indian food knowledge.
🔑 Real answer: Use Llama 3 (via Ollama or Groq's free API) and give it context — describe the dish with ingredients. AI knows ingredients well even if it doesn't know every dish name.

Example: "Pongal — rice, moong dal, ghee, pepper, cumin" → AI can estimate calories accurately from ingredients.

2. Voice Input for Calorie Guessing

This is your smartest differentiator. The flow:

User speaks → "I had two chapatis with dal and a small bowl of curd" → AI breaks it into components → estimates calories per item → gives total.

This works well with an LLM because you describe food naturally, not search a database. You avoid the database problem entirely for home-cooked Indian meals — exactly where MyFitnessPal fails Indians most.

3. Your TDEE Approach — Smart Simplification

  • Collect age, weight, height, gender, activity level once during onboarding
  • Calculate their daily calorie need (TDEE) and show it as a personal target
  • Each day: track via voice → show calories consumed vs. goal
  • No diet plans, no macro obsession — just awareness
✅ Less overwhelming than existing apps. Good instinct.

4. Web App → PWA → Mobile

Perfect sequence. Your real edge over MyFitnessPal for your audience:

  • Indian food accuracy — they fail here badly
  • Voice-first — no tedious searching and scrolling
  • Simple — built for real people, not fitness obsessives
